## Score-Based Causal Discovery on the Subspaces Themselves

This is the freshest angle. The score function approach to causal discovery — using \(\nabla \log p(X)\) to infer causal ordering without assuming Gaussian noise — was proven to work for additive noise models with essentially any noise distribution, and the June 2025 ICML paper by Montagna et al. extends it to linear, nonlinear, and latent variable settings simultaneously. The key insight for your paper is this: your regions have different intrinsic dimensionalities and different noise distributions (spiking is Poisson, not Gaussian). CD-NOD handles the heterogeneous-distribution problem across animals. But the score approach handles something different — it lets you infer causal ordering between regions from the geometry of the data distribution itself, without requiring parametric assumptions. Nobody has applied score-based causal discovery to neural population subspaces. The specific thing you would compute: treat each region's population activity projected onto its choice subspace as a variable in a causal graph, and ask what causal ordering between regions is implied by the score function across the 39 IBL animals. That gives you a data-driven causal circuit — not a correlation graph, not a connectivity matrix, but a causal ordering of regions in the choice computation — derived from geometry. That is a completely new claim about IBL data that nobody has made.[1][2][3]

## Causal Mixture Models for Region Types

The 2025 CISPA paper on Causal Mixture Models — where each observation is assigned to a latent subpopulation with its own structural causal model — is directly applicable to your heterogeneous brain region problem. Right now your geometric type taxonomy is a post-hoc classification: you run the experiments, then label regions. A causal mixture model treats the region type as a latent variable that you infer jointly with the causal structure. Applied to your data, the question becomes: is there a discrete latent variable indexing geometric regime (type 1 low-dim CKA-stable vs type 5 high-dim Procrustes-stable), and does that latent variable explain the distribution of CKA and Procrustes scores across regions? The 2025 JMLR paper on causal abstraction — Geiger, Icard, and collaborators — formalizes when a low-level system implements a high-level causal model via abstraction maps, and this has been the theoretical backbone of DAS and IIT in transformer circuits. You probably know this from your MI work. The novel thing here is applying it to biological circuits: does the mouse choice circuit in IBL implement the causal structure of a simple two-state accumulator model? You define the high-level causal model (prior × evidence → decision), define the candidate alignment (specific subspaces in specific regions align to prior, accumulated evidence, and decision variables), and then test it using interchange interventions — but in biology you don't get to do hard interventions, so you use the prior-block manipulation as a natural intervention on the prior variable. If swapping the prior-block context in region A's activity causes region B's activity to shift in the way the causal model predicts, the abstraction holds. ## What Causal Abstraction Actually Means Here

The causal abstraction framework asks: does your low-level system (neural populations in specific brain regions) implement a high-level causal model (a computational account of decision-making)? The test is the interchange intervention — you take two trials, swap the neural representation of one variable between them, and check if behavior changes as the high-level model predicts. In transformers you do this by patching activations. In biology your interchange interventions are natural — the IBL prior block manipulation is exactly this. When the prior switches from 80% left to 80% right, the brain's prior representation is being "patched" by the environment. You check whether downstream neural activity and behavior shift as your causal model predicts.[1][2][3]

The critical wrinkle: a 2024 paper proved that without the linearity constraint on alignment maps, you can find a spurious causal abstraction in literally any network including randomly initialized ones. Your Grassmannian geometry is exactly the right constraint to impose — the alignment map must be a rotation in Grassmannian space, not an arbitrary nonlinear transformation. That makes your geometric framework not just a comparison tool but the principled regularizer that makes causal abstraction non-trivial for biological data.[4]

## Candidate High-Level Causal Models to Test

These are real, published computational models of the IBL task where you can define specific variables, specific interventions, and specific predictions.

### Model 1: The Bayesian Accumulator

This is the canonical account. The high-level causal model has four variables: prior \(P(\text{left})\), sensory evidence \(e_t\), posterior belief \(b_t = P(\text{left}|e_{1:t})\), and choice \(c\). The causal structure is \(\text{prior} \to b_0\), then \(b_{t-1} + e_t \to b_t\), then \(b_T \to c\). The IBL September 2025 Nature paper showed that prior information was encoded across 20–30% of brain regions spanning from sensory to high-level cortex [3][5]. What nobody has done is ask which specific subspace in each region encodes which variable in this causal graph. You define: the alignment map sends prior-encoding subspace (estimated during the pre-stimulus window when only block identity differs) to the high-level prior variable, and the evidence-encoding subspace (estimated from stimulus-onset response) to the evidence variable. Then your interchange intervention is: take a trial from a left-prior block, swap in the prior subspace coordinates from a right-prior block trial, and see if the downstream choice subspace shifts. The natural block switch — which happens every ~50 trials — is your intervention. The prediction of the Bayesian accumulator is clean: swapping the prior subspace should shift the posterior subspace by a predictable rotation, and that rotation should be consistent across animals in Grassmannian distance even if not in raw coordinates.

### Model 2: The RL Synaptic-Weight Hypothesis

A 2021 arXiv paper from Krishnagopal and Latham proposed a completely different account of the IBL prior: the prior is stored in synaptic weights, not neural activity, and the neural activity difference between blocks is too small to decode. This is actually a testable negative prediction for your analysis. If the prior is stored in weights, you should find that the prior subspace Grassmannian distance across prior blocks is near zero — the subspace geometry does not change, only the activity magnitude does. If you find that the prior subspace actually does rotate between blocks (high Grassmannian distance between left-block and right-block pre-stimulus population activity), that is direct evidence against the synaptic-weight hypothesis and for the neural-activity hypothesis. This requires no new data — it is just computing Grassmannian distance between the choice subspace estimated on left-prior-block trials vs right-prior-block trials within each session.[6]

### Model 3: The Hidden Markov State Model

The 2024 biorXiv paper on IBL nonstationarity used an HMM with four behavioral states — two engaged (left bias, right bias) and two disengaged — and showed that mice switch between them in a predictable way based on reward and stimulus history. This gives you a richer causal model than just "left prior vs right prior." The high-level causal graph has: engagement state → strategy → choice, and reward history → state transition. For causal abstraction, the alignment question is: does any brain region's subspace geometry track engagement state independently of choice direction? A region that encodes engagement (not just left/right prior) would have a choice subspace that changes in a specific way during disengaged states — the subspace might collapse (lower effective dimensionality) or rotate to a different orientation. You can test this by splitting trials by behavioral state (using the published HMM classifications if you use the same IBL data) and computing the within-session Grassmannian distance between subspaces estimated in engaged vs disengaged trials. The prediction: regions that are part of the arousal/neuromodulatory circuit should show large engaged-vs-disengaged Grassmannian distances, while purely visual regions should show small ones.[7]

## What the Actual Experiment Looks Like

Concretely for Model 1, you would do this:

Step 1: for each session, estimate three subspaces — the prior subspace \(V_\text{prior}\) (LDA between left-block and right-block trials on pre-stimulus window), the evidence subspace \(V_\text{ev}\) (LDA between strong-left and strong-right contrast trials), and the choice subspace \(V_\text{choice}\) (LDA between left-choice and right-choice trials). These are your alignment maps from low-level neural activity to high-level causal variables.

Step 2: define the interchange intervention geometrically. Take the pre-stimulus population trajectory for a left-block trial, project it onto \(V_\text{prior}\), and replace those coordinates with the mean coordinates from right-block trials. Predict what the choice-time population activity should look like under the Bayesian accumulator model. The prediction is that the posterior subspace should shift toward the right-choice attractor.

Step 3: test the prediction across sessions. Does the Grassmannian distance between \(V_\text{prior}\) and \(V_\text{choice}\) across sessions predict behavioral prior sensitivity (the shift of the psychometric curve between blocks)? Regions where \(d_G(V_\text{prior}, V_\text{choice})\) is small should be "prior-to-choice routing" regions; regions where it is large should be regions that encode prior and choice in separate subspaces.

Step 4: the key causal abstraction test. Compute interchange intervention accuracy: how often does swapping prior subspace coordinates between left-block and right-block trials cause the downstream choice subspace to flip to the correct side? This is your IIA analog from DAS, but implemented in geometry rather than activation patching.

## Why This Is Genuinely Novel

The DAS/causal abstraction literature has been applied only to artificial neural networks so far. The biological neuroscience literature has never used interchange interventions as an analysis tool — it has used lesion studies, optogenetics, and stimulation, but not the computational notion of counterfactual swapping of representational subspaces. The connection you can make is this: your Grassmannian framework provides the only principled way to define "same subspace" across animals and sessions, which is the prerequisite for interchange interventions to be meaningful in biological data. Without it, swapping activations between animals is incoherent (different neurons). With Grassmannian geometry, you can define a swap as "transport the subspace coordinates along the geodesic to the other animal's reference frame." That is the technical contribution that makes this approach original, and it directly draws on everything already in your paper.[2][8][1]

The connection to your geometric type taxonomy is the final piece. Your hypothesis is: CKA-type regions (low dimensionality, linearly stable) will have high IIA for simple linear causal variables like prior, while Procrustes-type regions (high dimensionality, geometry-stable but not linearly stable) will have high IIA only for nonlinear causal variables like engagement state or strategy. That prediction ties the entire paper together: your metric comparison, your dimensionality mediation result, your geometric type taxonomy, and the causal abstraction analysis all converge on the same story about which computational operations different cortical regions support.

## The Closest Thing to DAS You Can Actually Do Without Patching

Here's the precise procedure for doing DAS-style analysis with natural interventions:

You define a high-level causal model — say, the two-variable model: prior \(P\) causes choice \(C\). You define your alignment maps as rotations: \(R_P: \mathbb{R}^k \to \{-1, +1\}\) projects the k-dimensional prior subspace onto a scalar prior estimate, and \(R_C\) does the same for choice. The interchange intervention test is: does the correlation between \(R_P\) and \(R_C\) across trials correctly predict the behavioral prior effect, and does it do so region-specifically? You compute this for every region and get a map of "which regions show the causal prior→choice geometry predicted by the model." That map is your biological DAS result.

The key methodological novelty over what already exists in the IBL literature: previous papers ask whether you can decode prior from a region. That is a purely associational claim. Your analysis asks whether the prior subspace geometry in a region is causally organized relative to the choice subspace geometry downstream — which requires the Grassmannian distance between \(V_\text{prior}\) and \(V_\text{choice}\) to be predictive of behavioral causal sensitivity, not just decoding accuracy. A region could have high prior decodability but low causal role — it encodes prior as a side effect but doesn't use it for choice. Your geometry test distinguishes these cases and the decoding approach cannot.

## One More Angle: The Geiger et al. Boundedness Result

The 2024 paper showed causal abstraction is trivially satisfied without the linearity constraint. But they also showed the converse: with a sufficiently constrained alignment map, failed causal abstraction is informative — it tells you the causal structure of the high-level model is wrong, not just that the alignment was too flexible. Your Grassmannian rotation constraint is exactly the right constraint. So if your IIA metric comes back low — the prior subspace geometry does not predict choice subspace geometry the way the Bayesian accumulator says it should — that is not a failure of your method. It is a result about the animal's actual causal circuit structure. You can say: the Bayesian accumulator's causal structure is not implemented in MOs (low IIA), but it is implemented in ACA (high IIA), which gives you a specific anatomical localization of which step of the accumulator is where. That is the kind of result that ends up in Nature Neuroscience, not just a methods paper.[5]
