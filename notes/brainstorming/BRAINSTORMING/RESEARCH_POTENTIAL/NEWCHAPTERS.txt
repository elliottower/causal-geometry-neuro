### 4. Comprehensive Analysis

You can absolutely turn “mechanistic validity” into a general-purpose engine for science, not just an interpretability philosophy piece. All three models converged on the same core reframing: MechVal is a validity ledger for mechanistic claims, and that ledger is useful anywhere you have expensive experiments, noisy measurements, and people overclaiming “mechanism” from correlations. In other words, MechVal is not tied to transformers; it’s tied to the epistemic structure of mechanism claims (construct → measurement → intervention → scope), which is exactly what your paper formalizes. Once you say it that way, drug discovery becomes an obvious target: the entire industry is “claim mechanisms from messy evidence, then pay billions if you’re wrong.” The models also agree that Lila Sciences is structurally aligned: they publicly describe an autonomous-science loop that generates hypotheses, designs experiments, runs them, and learns. Any loop like that has a missing component: deciding which claims are “ready” versus “still correlational,” and choosing the next experiment to upgrade the evidential tier. That’s basically MechVal as an algorithmic planning objective.[1][2][4]

The cleanest way to make this real (and credible to both grants and jobs) is to ground it in public benchmark ecosystems that already contain interventions. That’s why all three models independently pointed at single-cell perturbation platforms. Open Problems in Single-Cell Analysis has become a community benchmarking and competition hub with public perturbation tasks. CausalBench is even more directly your lane: it exists specifically to evaluate causal network inference using large-scale perturbational single-cell data. This matters because it bypasses the “observational ceiling” problem you’ve been wrestling with in neuroscience: in perturb-seq style datasets, the intervention is real (gene knockdown/knockout), so you can operationalize MechVal criteria like intervention reach, specificity, mediation, transfer, and dose-response in a way that’s impossible in pure recordings. And it also gives you something funders love: an evaluation harness.[5][7][8][9]

Where the models diverge is mostly strategy and emphasis. Claude Opus 4.7 pushes a Lila-first pitch: autonomous labs need a validity calculus now, so sell MechVal as the missing decision layer and make the tiers probabilistic/uncertainty-aware. GPT-5.5 pushes “prove it on public benchmarks first,” especially Open Problems perturbation prediction and therapeutic benchmarks, then walk into Lila with a demo and a story. Gemini 3.1 leans hardest into your Jensen heritage: treat biology/neuroscience as relational causal systems, and use relational causal discovery ideas to formalize entity types and relations rather than treating everything as iid. These are compatible: you can do the benchmark demo as the proof artifact, but frame the intellectual core in Jensen language (causal query, identifiability assumptions, relational schema), and then pitch the whole thing as “validity-aware autonomous science” to Lila/Boston biotech.[6][14][15]

On the grants side, the strongest near-term fit surfaced by Claude is ARPA-H’s IGoR program: it explicitly calls for mechanistic disease models encoding causal biological relationships and an AI orchestration layer that identifies knowledge gaps and designs optimal experiments. That is almost literally MechVal plus an experiment-selection agent. The practical catch is: you typically need a team with wet-lab/robotics/protocol capability to be competitive, but you can be the “validity + causal modeling + evaluation” lead inside such a team. In parallel, GPT-5.5’s “MechVal-TDC” idea is a smart productizable angle: Therapeutics Data Commons (and TDC-2) is already an ecosystem of tasks/benchmarks in drug ML. Adding a validity layer (“mechanistic validity cards”) turns your work into a plug-in evaluation standard rather than a one-off paper.[16][17][18]

What would David Jensen tell you? Based on his stated agenda—causal inference for large interacting systems and methods for constructing causal models rather than just predictive models—he’d push you to be ruthless about formalization: define unit of analysis, treatment/intervention, outcome, population, and the identification assumptions before you touch geometry. He’d likely also push you toward settings where identifiability is provable or at least testable (held-out interventions, domain shifts, relational constraints), which is exactly why perturbation single-cell benchmarks (CausalBench/Open Problems) and intervention-scored challenges (DREAM-style) are so strategically valuable.[8][11][12][19][5]

Recommendations: If you want the fastest path to something fundable and job-relevant in Boston biotech, build one tight demo in perturbation biology: a MechVal-style audit layer on top of a perturbation benchmark (CausalBench or Open Problems perturbation prediction) that outputs (a) an explicit causal claim object, (b) which validity criteria it satisfies/fails, and (c) the next best experiment to upgrade the claim. Then pitch that as “validity-aware autonomous discovery” to Lila (Cambridge), and separately as a grantable infrastructure component to ARPA-H IGoR–shaped teams.[2][5][8][16]
