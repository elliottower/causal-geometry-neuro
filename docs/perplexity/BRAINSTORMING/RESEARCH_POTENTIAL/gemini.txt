•	Why you have an edge: Everyone else is using standard statistical constraint-based (PC, FCI) or score-based causal discovery. But you know that genes, like neurons, don’t act individually—they act in low-dimensional, distributed subspaces. You can apply your Grassmannian geometric approach to identify the causal subspaces of gene expression, rather than trying to build a massive, noisy graph of individual genes.
2. Relational Causal Discovery (The David Jensen Angle)
Since you worked with David Jensen at UMass Amherst, you have a distinct advantage. Jensen’s lab pioneered Relational Causal Discovery (RCD).
•	What Jensen would say: He would tell you that assuming data is independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) is the original sin of both neuroscience and biology. Neurons aren’t i.i.d.; they are part of a spatial and functional network. Genes aren’t i.i.d.; they exist in pathways.
•	How to use it: Jensen’s Abstract Ground Graphs (AGGs) allow you to learn causal models from relational data (e.g., Region A connects to Region B, Cell Type X interacts with Cell Type Y). You should fuse your geometric subspace work with Jensen’s relational causal discovery. The claim: “We don’t just find the causal subspace; we use Relational Causal Discovery to show how the geometric properties of one region causally determine the geometry of downstream regions.” This is a massive methodological flex that almost no one in standard ML or bio knows how to do.
