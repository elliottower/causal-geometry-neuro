Open challenges / benchmarks you can actually use
1. Open Problems in Single-Cell Analysis
This is probably the cleanest “challenge” ecosystem for you. Open Problems is a living benchmark platform for single-cell methods, with 81 public datasets, 171 methods, 12 core tasks, and 37 metrics, and it has powered multiple NeurIPS competitions [lila +2]. There was a major Open Problems single-cell perturbation challenge where the task was predicting how small molecules change gene expression in different cell types [engineering.yale +2].
Your unique contribution: add a MechVal-style evaluation layer to perturbation prediction.
Most methods answer: “Can I predict the post-drug expression vector?”
You ask: “Does the model’s proposed mechanism survive validity tests?”
For example:
Does the inferred drug mechanism transfer across cell types?
Does it distinguish direct target effects from downstream correlated expression?
Does it pass counterfactual consistency under held-out perturbations?
Does it recover known pathway structure, or just exploit batch/cell-type confounds?
Does it make useful experiment-selection recommendations?
That is a stronger scientific question than leaderboard RMSE. It is very Jensen-coded: stop optimizing predictive score; define the causal estimand, the selection mechanism, the bias structure, the intervention regime, and the generalization target.
2. CausalBench / gene regulatory network inference
CausalBench is a benchmark for gene regulatory network inference using large-scale single-cell perturbation data, designed specifically because synthetic causal benchmarks do not reflect real biological settings [semanticscholar +1]. This is basically begging for your thing.
The standard task is: infer edges in a gene regulatory network.
Your twist: evaluate inferred edges by mechanistic validity tier.
A gene edge “A causes B” is not one thing. It could mean:
A is predictive of B observationally.
A perturbation changes B.
A’s effect on B is direct rather than mediated.
A’s effect is stable across cell types.
A’s effect is dose-dependent.
A’s effect survives adjustment for cell state, batch, cell cycle, and latent differentiation trajectory.
Those are MechVal criteria in biology clothing. You could write a methods paper: “Mechanistic Validity for Gene Regulatory Network Claims.” It would be incredibly natural.
3. DREAM challenges / HPN-DREAM cancer network inference
The DREAM5 challenge benchmarked over 30 methods for transcriptional network inference on organisms like E. coli, S. aureus, yeast, and synthetic systems [zitniklab.hms.harvard]. The HPN-DREAM breast cancer network challenge scored inferred causal networks against unseen interventional data in cancer cell lines . This is gold for you because it already has the Jensen-style structure: train on some perturbations, test on held-out interventions.
Your contribution: take the old challenge submissions or modern methods and analyze failure modes using MechVal categories.
Instead of “method A got AUROC 0.62,” ask:
Which validity criterion does each method fail?
Does it fail internal validity because it confounds common causes?
Does it fail external validity because it does not transfer across cell lines?
Does it fail discriminant validity because it cannot distinguish pathway neighbors?
Does it fail construct validity because “edge” is not operationally defined consistently?
This is a great low-cost paper because the data and historical baselines already exist.

Drug discovery as MechVal
Yes, drug discovery is absolutely a MechVal problem.
A drug discovery claim usually sounds like:
Compound X treats disease Y by modulating target/pathway Z.
That contains at least four separable claims:
1.	The compound binds or affects target Z.
2.	Target Z causally affects disease-relevant phenotype Y.
3.	The compound’s beneficial effect is mediated by Z, not off-targets.
4.	The mechanism generalizes across relevant biological contexts: cell types, patient subgroups, disease stages, species, assay platforms.
Most AI drug discovery models only support claim 1-ish or a weak version of claim 2. They predict binding, expression response, morphology, or clinical outcome. But they do not prove the mechanism.
So your validity tiers could be:
Proposed: model predicts compound-target or compound-phenotype association.
Associationally supported: association replicates across datasets.
Perturbationally supported: genetic or chemical perturbation moves phenotype.
Mediation-supported: target/pathway mediates compound effect.
Discriminantly supported: similar phenotype but different mechanism is separated; similar target but different phenotype is separated.
Translationally supported: mechanism transfers across cell type, organoid, animal, and patient data.
Triangulated: multiple independent evidence families agree: binding assay, CRISPR perturbation, transcriptomics, imaging, pathway prior, dose-response, rescue experiment.
That is a product. Not just a paper.
What David Jensen would probably tell you
Based on the Jensen-style causal inference instinct, he would probably be excited but would slap the vague “mechanism” language out of it.
He’d say something like:
First define the unit, treatment, outcome, time, and population. Then define the causal query. Then tell me what assumptions identify it.
For neuroscience:
Unit: session, animal, trial, neuron, region?
Treatment: perturbing a subspace? choosing a metric? optogenetic inhibition? task condition?
Outcome: decoding accuracy, behavior, activity geometry, future region activity?
Population: Steinmetz mice? IBL mice? all mammals? decision tasks only?
For drug discovery:
Unit: cell, cell line, patient, assay, perturbation condition?
Treatment: drug, dose, genetic knockout, pathway activation?
Outcome: expression shift, morphology, viability, disease phenotype?
Population: one cell line or clinical subgroup?
He would also warn you not to confuse predictive representation with causal structure. A graph neural network over a connectome or gene network is not causal just because it has edges. A high CKA score is not mechanism similarity. A drug embedding cluster is not mechanism of action. The causal claim requires a counterfactual: what would change under intervention?
But he would probably like the MechVal angle because it forces the claim to be explicit and auditably wrong. That is very Jensen: make the hypothesis graph explicit, track assumptions, test generalization under shifts, and use held-out interventions when possible.
He’d also probably push you toward relational/selection-bias questions rather than fancy geometry first:
Why did these samples get observed?
Which neurons/cells/drugs/targets are missing?
Are you conditioning on post-treatment variables?
Are perturbations randomized or chosen by an adaptive policy?
Are cell lines exchangeable?
Are assays comparable?
Does a model trained on one experimental design generalize to another?
In other words: geometry gives you invariants; causal inference tells you whether those invariants mean anything.
What causal inference stuff you should actually be doing
The highest-value methods for you are not generic DAGs. They are:
1. Transportability and external validity
This is huge for both neuroscience and drug discovery.
Question: when does a mechanism found in one context transfer to another?
Neuroscience:
mouse A to mouse B
Steinmetz to IBL
MOs to ACA
choice task to navigation task
Drug discovery:
cell line to primary tissue
in vitro to in vivo
mouse to human
healthy to disease
one patient subgroup to another
Your geometry paper is already secretly a transportability paper. CKA/UMAP/Grassmannian are not just similarity metrics; they are candidate transportability diagnostics. The better framing:
Which geometric invariants predict transportability of neural or biological mechanisms?
That is much more fundable.
2. Invariant causal prediction
Find features whose relationship to outcome stays stable across environments. This maps perfectly to “mechanistic features.”
In neuroscience: a choice subspace is more mechanistic if its relationship to behavior is invariant across animals, labs, stimulus contrasts, and behavioral states.
In drug discovery: a pathway is more likely causal if its relationship to phenotype is invariant across cell lines, perturbation modalities, and assay platforms.
This is a natural bridge between MechVal and causal inference: invariance is observational evidence that raises a claim’s validity tier, even without direct intervention.
3. Negative controls and falsification tests
This is underrated and very Jensen.
For every claimed mechanism, define variables it should not predict or conditions where it should disappear.
Neuroscience:
A choice subspace should not predict irrelevant movement artifacts after controlling for licking.
A visual stimulus manifold should weaken in nonvisual regions or pre-stimulus windows.
A claimed topological loop should vanish under time-shuffle or trial-label shuffle.
Drug discovery:
A drug mechanism should not appear in an unrelated pathway.
A target effect should vanish under rescue.
A chemical perturbation should not match a genetic knockout if the target is wrong.
This is exactly how you can make observational evidence stronger without intervention: falsify plausible alternatives.
4. Proximal causal inference / latent confounding
Biology is confounder hell. You rarely observe all causes.
Proximal causal inference uses proxy variables to adjust for hidden confounding. This could be huge in single-cell and neuroscience:
cell state proxies for unobserved differentiation state
behavioral variables proxy for arousal/movement
batch variables proxy for lab/assay artifacts
gene modules proxy for unobserved pathway activation
You could build MechVal criteria around whether a claim survives proximal adjustment.
5. Causal representation learning
This is your natural home. The goal is to learn latent variables that correspond to causal factors, not just predictive factors.
Your geometric type idea is basically a causal representation learning diagnostic:
A representation is more likely mechanistic if it is invariant across environments, predictive under interventions, disentangled from known confounds, and transportable.
This applies equally to neural manifolds, cell embeddings, drug embeddings, and LLM internals.
