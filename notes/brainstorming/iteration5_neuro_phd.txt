This changes the paper significantly — and in a good direction. Here is the honest deep assessment of what would make a neuroscience PhD actually engage with this.

## The Question They Already Care About

The deepest result you have is not "CKA and Procrustes disagree." That is a methods paper. The deep result is this: effective dimensionality determines which similarity metric is informative about cross-animal circuit preservation. That is a claim about how the brain organizes computation across the cortical hierarchy, and neuroscientists have been fighting about exactly this for the last five years.[1][2]

The 2024 Stringer-adjacent paper showing that dimensionality scales unboundedly with neuron number — with half the variance in 16 behavioral dimensions but an ever-growing tail of internal dimensions — is directly relevant. And the recent finding that high-dimensional regions generalize worse across domains while low-dimensional regions generalize better connects dimensionality to the thing neuroscientists actually care about: what makes a circuit computation transferable. Your paper has something to say here that is empirically grounded: regions with flat eigenspectra (high intrinsic dimensionality) look linearly dissimilar across animals (low CKA) even when their manifold geometry is conserved (high Procrustes). That is an observation about how the cortical hierarchy handles between-animal variability — low-dimensional sensory regions are more linearly aligned, high-dimensional association regions preserve shape but not orientation.[2][3]

## The Specific Thing a Neuroscience PhD Would Want You To Do

Right now the paper maps brain regions to a CKA × Procrustes scatter plot and shows the anti-correlation is mediated by dimensionality. To a neuroscientist that is interesting but abstract. What they want to know is: does this map onto anything anatomical or functional they already believe?

The cortical hierarchy runs from primary sensory (low dimensionality, high CKA, low Procrustes distance in your data) to association cortex (high dimensionality, low CKA, preserved manifold shape). That gradient — sensory to transmodal — is one of the most replicated findings in systems neuroscience, showing up in timescale gradients, myelination gradients, laminar complexity gradients, and representational generalization gradients. If your geometric type taxonomy aligns with this gradient, you have a result neuroscientists will immediately contextualize as meaningful and not just a metric comparison.[4][5][6]

The experiment to run: take your region-level (CKA, Procrustes, effective dimensionality) coordinates and regress them against the Mesoscale Mouse Connectome hierarchy position. Or more simply against whether regions are primary sensory, secondary sensory/motor, or association. If high-dimensionality flat-spectrum regions cluster in association cortex and low-dimensionality steep-spectrum regions cluster in primary sensory/motor cortex, you have turned a methods paper into a paper about cortical organization that the neuroscience community will actually remember.

## The Three Deepest Things You Can Add

First, connect the dimensionality type to the cortical hierarchy explicitly. Even a simple ANOVA across three tiers (primary, secondary, association) would let you say "geometric type is not random variation across regions — it tracks the canonical sensory-to-association hierarchy." That is two paragraphs and one figure and it transforms the paper's reception.[5][1]

Second, the partial correlation sign flip has a theoretical prediction that nobody has tested: if effective dimensionality is the mediator, then you can predict which metric will be more reliable for a given region before measuring it. High-dimensional region (flat spectrum)? Use Procrustes, not CKA. Low-dimensional region (steep spectrum)? CKA is a reasonable proxy. That is a practical recommendation for the whole field, and it comes directly from your mediation analysis. No additional experiment needed — you can derive it from exp24 results plus a clear explanatory figure.[7]

Third, there is a specific finite-sampling confound you should address directly because it will be the first thing a careful reviewer raises. A 2025 NeurIPS paper applied Random Matrix Theory to show that CKA is systematically underestimated under finite neuron sampling due to eigenvector delocalization, with the bias depending on the spectral properties of the representation. The key point is that flat-spectrum (high-dimensional) regions are the most biased toward low CKA under finite sampling — which is exactly the pattern you observe. You need to check whether your dimensionality mediation result survives after applying their denoising correction, or at least show that the bias goes in the wrong direction to explain your result. If correcting for finite-sampling bias still gives you the anti-correlation, that is the strongest possible version of your robustness story and would satisfy any neuroscientist with methods training.[8]

## What Would Actually Make Them Trust It

The current controls — sub-sampling, jackknife, HSIC test — are convincing to an ML audience. A neuroscientist will also want to see one of these: task-specificity (does the anti-correlation weaken for trial-shuffled data, i.e., is it about the task structure and not just the resting-state noise covariance?), or behavioral prediction (do regions that are more "Procrustes-type" predict choice behavior more accurately with a nonlinear decoder relative to a linear one?). The second one is your exp22 prediction test and it is the most compelling possible bridge between the geometry story and function. If kNN-over-LDA advantage correlates with CKA in the expected direction across the full 73-region run, that is the experiment that turns the paper from a comparison of metrics into a claim about what kind of computation each geometric type supports.[9]

The one-sentence version of what the paper should say is: whether a brain region uses linear or nonlinear geometry for cross-animal circuit preservation is predictable from its position in the cortical dimensionality hierarchy, and that geometric type determines which computational readout the circuit supports. That is a sentence a neuroscience PhD would find worth arguing about at a conference, which is exactly where you want to be.
